RoadAid AI — catching incidents in seconds, not minutes

A computer-vision system that watches existing motorway cameras and flags accidents, debris and breakdowns the moment they happen — compressing the window before a secondary incident forms.

OpenCVCNNTensorFlow PythonIoTPersonal build · GitHub

The business problem

On a motorway, the expensive number is not the crash — it's the time between the crash and someone knowing about it. Today that detection usually depends on a person: an operator noticing a camera feed, or a driver calling it in. That lag runs to minutes.

Minutes matter more than they sound. A stopped vehicle in a live lane forms a queue, and the queue is where the secondary incident happens — often worse than the first, because it arrives at speed into stationary traffic. Compress the detection time and you compress the whole response chain: signage, lane closure, responder dispatch.

The thesis: the value isn't the detection itself — camera analytics aren't new. The value is the minutes it removes from the front of the incident-response timeline.

Architecture

The design goal was deliberately unglamorous: use the cameras that already exist. No new roadside capital, no bespoke sensors — just a processing layer on the existing estate.

OpenCV handles frame capture and pre-processing (resizing, background modelling, region-of-interest masking so the model isn't distracted by irrelevant motion). A CNN classifies each region into normal traffic vs incident categories — stopped vehicle, debris, pedestrian, wrong-way — with a confidence score. Detections above threshold raise an alert; borderline cases route to a human rather than firing automatically.

Workflow

  • Ingest: pull frames from an existing feed at a sampling rate that balances latency against compute.
  • Pre-process: mask to the carriageway, normalise for lighting and weather, subtract static background.
  • Classify: CNN scores each region of interest; incident classes vs normal flow.
  • Gate: high-confidence detections alert immediately; low-confidence ones are queued for a human glance, not dropped.
  • Hand off: a confirmed detection becomes a structured event a control room can act on — not just a pixel highlight.

What was hard

The model was the easy part. The honest lessons were all at the edges:

  • False positives destroy trust faster than false negatives. An operator who gets three bad alerts stops looking at the fourth. Tuning the confidence gate — and routing uncertainty to a human instead of the alarm — mattered more than raw accuracy.
  • The world is not the training set. Rain, glare, night, a camera knocked slightly off-axis — each is a distribution shift. Pre-processing and ROI masking did more for real-world robustness than a bigger model would have.
  • Latency is a budget. Every stage — capture, preprocess, inference — spends milliseconds you're trying to save. The point of the whole system is speed, so the pipeline has to stay lean.

What I learned

The recurring lesson of roadside AI: the detection is solvable; the integration is the project. A model that fires in a notebook is not a system. A system is the camera estate, the network, the confidence gating, and — above all — a handoff into a workflow a human trusts enough to act on. That last part is where these programs live or die.

It also reset my sense of where the barrier actually is. Most operators assume the technical difficulty is high and the organisational lift is low. In practice it's the reverse: the CV is within reach today, and the hard, expensive, multi-year part is the plumbing into operations.

Future improvements

  • Track objects across frames (not just per-frame classification) to cut flicker and confirm persistence before alerting.
  • Edge deployment (containerised) so inference runs near the camera rather than shipping frames.
  • A feedback loop: every operator confirm/reject becomes a labelled example that improves the gate.
