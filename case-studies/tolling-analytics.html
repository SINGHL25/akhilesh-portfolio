Finding the fault the road never shows

Free-flow tolling fails silently. This is how billions of passage records became an assurance layer that catches a classification sensor drifting for days — before it surfaces weeks later as a disputed invoice.

The business problem

A barrier tolling system is brutally honest: when it breaks, the arm stays down, the driver stops, and the fault reports itself in seconds. Multi-lane free-flow has no such courtesy. When a roadside unit drifts out of tolerance, or image quality degrades past the plate reader's limit, or a classification sensor starts mis-binning heavy vehicles, nothing visible happens at the lane. Traffic keeps flowing at 100 km/h. The revenue quietly stops being right.

The consequence surfaces weeks downstream, at the invoice — by which point you've lost both the transaction and some of the customer's trust. On a free-flow network the driver can't verify anything at the point of use, so the assumption that the invoice is correct is the product.

The core issue: the failures that matter aren't outages — they're degradations. A drifting RSU, deteriorating image quality, a mis-binning sensor: all of them still report as "up". The system is up. It's also wrong, on every vehicle, until someone goes looking.

Why "check the logs" stops working

The instinctive answer is to review the logs. That works for one vehicle. It does not work at scale. On a network of any size, billions of passage and vehicle-classification records accumulate. A human reading log files can confirm what happened to a single passage. A human cannot see that one classification sensor has been mis-binning heavy vehicles at a slightly elevated rate for eleven days.

That's not a diligence problem you can fix with effort. It's a scale problem, and scale problems need tooling.

The pipeline

The log analyser automates the parsing and normalisation of complex tolling logs, then computes rolling baselines over the passage data: classification mix, tag-read rates, exception rates, per-lane distributions. Anything that drifts away from its own baseline — not a fixed threshold, but its own recent history — gets flagged. Power BI turns the same aggregates into performance insight for asset strategy teams; the flags feed straight into root cause analysis.

The signal that matters most

For a whole class of free-flow fault, the transaction data is the only place the fault is visible. The hardware looks fine. The logs, read one line at a time, look fine. The pattern in the aggregate doesn't — a truck-class ratio quietly sitting well below its expected band is a mis-binning sensor announcing itself, if anything is listening. The headline number is the ~30% cut in manual effort. But the bigger win wasn't the hours saved — it was seeing patterns that manual review structurally could not surface, because no human reads eleven days of classification data looking for a two-percent drift.

What I learned

  • Budget for the pipeline, not just the portal. The roadside hardware is a procurement exercise with a comparison table. The data-assurance layer behind it decides whether the revenue is right — and it's almost always designed last.
  • RCA that stops at the component isn't RCA. "Replaced the unit" tells you nothing about the other units drifting the same way. The analytics exist to turn one fault into a question about the whole estate.
  • Anomaly detection beats thresholds. A fixed limit misses slow drift and cries wolf on legitimate variation. Comparing each signal to its own recent baseline is what catches the quiet failures.
The credibility of the next generation of road pricing — distance-based charging, GNSS tolling, road-user charging as fuel excise declines — rests on today's operators being demonstrably accurate without the driver's supervision. That accuracy is earned in exactly this kind of unglamorous assurance work.

Future improvements

  • Predictive layer: move from "this drifted" to "this is trending toward drift" using the same baselines.
  • Automated RCA prompts — when a flag fires, surface the correlated assets and recent config changes automatically.
  • Tie anomaly flags directly into the CMMS so a detection opens a job with its evidence attached.
